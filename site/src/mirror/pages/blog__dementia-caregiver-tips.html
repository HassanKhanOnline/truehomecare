How to Help Someone with Dementia: 13 Dementia Caregiver Tips

Saima Adil Zafar

20 September, 2024

Dementia is a challenging condition not only for the one diagnosed with it but also for their loved ones.  If you are caring for your loved one with dementia, you might feel that it is both a rewarding experience and a difficult task. But don’t worry, you are not alone. There are many practical tips for caregivers of dementia that are going to make caregiving easy. With these Dementia caregivers tips, you’ll be able to provide the emotional and physical support needed by your loved one without feeling overwhelmed. 

What is Dementia?

Dementia is a general term that refers to the progressive worsening of cognitive abilities, such as memory, reasoning, and thinking. This decline in cognitive ability is severe enough to interfere with daily life and activities. This is not a disease; it is rather a condition that is characterized by a group of symptoms associated with underlying conditions such as vascular dementia, Alzheimer’s disease, frontotemporal dementia, or Lewy body dementia.

Other than the loss of memory, an individual suffering from dementia may even experience difficulty with language and communication, change in behavior, mood swings, and a decline in the ability to perform basic day-to-day tasks. These symptoms worsen with time and affect an individual’s ability to live independently and perform daily tasks. 

13 Dementia Caregivers Tips

Here are a few tips for dementia caregivers that can make the caregiving process a whole lot easier and stress-free. 

Tip # 1: Understand Dementia & Be Patient with Your Loved One

Caring for someone with dementia requires you to be patient and understanding. You need to remember at all times that memory loss, confusion, and behavioral changes are a part of this condition. Hence, it is important as a caregiver not to get frustrated but to stay calm and patient at all times when dealing with your loved one with dementia. Remember that there is always a reason behind the behavior of a person suffering from dementia. Your loved one may not remember our name or may even forget routine tasks, but if you are patient, you can maintain their dignity and trust.

Tip # 2: Create a Safe and Comfortable Environment

A safe, comfortable, and familiar environment is essential to keep your loved one calm. Your loved one is going to feel more secure and less anxious in a familiar environment. Make adjustments to your home and remove items that pose a potential hazard to reduce the risk of injuries from falls. Consider labeling items; this will help your loved one navigate, move around, and live more independently. Remember to focus on comfort; simple things like familiar items and pleasant surroundings can reduce anxiety. 

Tip # 3: Stick to a Routine

Another significant dementia tip for caregivers is sticking to a routine. Remember that for individuals with dementia, familiarity brings comfort. Following a consistent routine will help reduce anxiety and confusion for individuals with dementia. Scheduling meals, activities, and even nap times at regular intervals can make the day predictable and manageable for both you and your loved one.

Tip # 4: Use Simple & Clear Language to Communicate

If your loved one is suffering from dementia, they may find following complex instructions difficult. Hence, you need to communicate clearly and in simple language. Using these communication tips for dementia caregivers will make the process easier:

  1. Ask simple questions, preferably ones with simple yes and no answers. 
  2. Avoid asking open-ended questions, as your loved one might not know how to communicate what they feel. 
  3. Speak to your loved one in a positive and respectful tone. 
  4. When you are talking to them, try and limit noise and distractions. 
  5. Use simple words and sentences, and speak slowly and reassuringly.
  6. Oftentimes, your loved one might not understand what you are asking, and you might need to repeat yourself. When doing so, repeat your question in the same tone and use the same words; don’t get agitated or frustrated. If your loved ones are still not able to understand, rephrase your question in simpler words.
  7. Don’t use pronouns; use names instead. 

Tip # 5: Encourage Your Loved One to Participate in Physical Activities and Exercise

Exercise can be beneficial for individuals with dementia. It not only improves their mood but also helps with their overall health and cognitive function. Depending on the ability of your loved one, you should encourage them to be involved in simple physical activities, whether it is the simple task of stretching, walking, or gardening. 

Exercise can help manage stress, improve the quality of sleep, and reduce agitation, and if you participate in physical activities with your loved ones, it will even strengthen your bond with them.  

Tip # 6: Provide Emotional Support & Reassurance

Living with dementia can be confusing and frightening for your loved ones, so you have to be there by their side, providing emotional support and reassurance. Spending time together, doing activities they enjoy – whether it be going out for a walk, listening to music together, or looking at old photos – can be comforting. Sometimes, just being there for them is enough.

Tip # 7: Support Daily Activities without Taking Over

It is important that you support your loved one with daily activities, but remember to find a balance. A balance that maintains their independence while you are providing assistance when and where needed. Offer them the support they need with daily activities, such as cooking, bathing, dressing, etc., but let them do the tasks they can do on their own. This will help maintain their dignity and confidence.

Tip # 8: Be Proactive Rather Than Reactive

Dementia is a progressive disease; the symptoms may worsen over time, and hence, it is important for you to proactively track these changes. Identify when certain behaviors are becoming a pattern and decide what you can do to resolve or manage these symptoms. If you are being reactive, handling the situation might become difficult; if you are only delaying making the right decisions, your options might become limited moving forward, and it can also pose a safety concern for your loved one. 

Tip # 9: Learn To Manage Behavioral Changes

As dementia progresses, you may notice changes in the behavior of your loved one, such as increased agitation, wandering, or aggression. These changes are part of the condition and can get challenging to deal with. Following caregiver tips for dementia can help you manage behavioral changes. 

  1. If your loved one becomes upset, try to remain calm, accommodate their behavior, or divert their attention to an enjoyable, more calming activity. 
  2. Avoid arguing or reasoning with them, as this may only escalate the situation.
  3. If your loved one has dementia, they might not be able to tell you what they need. Hence, it is important to understand what needs they might be trying to fulfill by such behavior. 
  4. It is also essential to keep in mind that what works today may not even work tomorrow, so you might need to be creative and flexible with your solutions.

Tip # 10: Take Steps to Avoid Agitation, Conflict and Stress

When caring for a loved one with dementia, try to avoid situations that might give rise to agitation, conflict, and stress. Rather than arguing with your loved one, it is important to work toward diffusing the situation. 

It is also essential to remember that your loved one might feel agitated and confused due to changes in the routine. Hence, it is important to maintain a routine for individuals with dementia. 

Tip # 11: Take Care of Yourself As Well

Caregiving can be all-consuming, and it’s easy to forget to take care of yourself. Family caregivers to take a break while ensuring their loved ones continue to receive quality care. Taking time for yourself to recharge isn’t selfish—it’s necessary for your own health and wellbeing. True Homecare provides respite care services so you can rest and return to caregiving refreshed. 

Tip # 12: Opt for Professional Help When Needed

Although dementia care coming from the family is valuable, sometimes you might feel like you need a break. Don’t hesitate to look for professional support when needed; if you are looking for someone who can truly provide quality home care, check out True Homecare. Tip # 13: Plan for the Future

It is important to remember that at some point in the future, you might need to make health care decisions for your loved one. Making healthcare decisions can be overwhelming; hence, we recommend discussing what your loved one wants with you well in advance. 

Over To You

In order to fulfill caregivers’ duties and responsibilities, you need to learn about your loved one’s condition and know what to expect as the condition progresses. With these dementia caregivers tips, you’ll be ready to deal with challenging situations, offering meaningful care while also maintaining your wellbeing.

FAQs

Q: What should I do when my loved one with dementia becomes agitated?

A: If your loved one becomes agitated, the first thing you need to remember is to stay calm. Try to divert their attention to more calming and soothing activities. Do not argue or reason with them, as this will only make them more agitated.  

Q: How can I balance caregiving and my own self-care?

A: Caregiving can be an all-consuming process. Hence, it is important to take breaks and opt for professional care as and when needed to maintain your wellbeing while ensuring that your loved one is cared for. 

Q: How can I improve communication with someone who has dementia?

A: To improve communication with a loved one with dementia, you need to use simple language and short sentences. Don’t ask open-ended questions; rather, ask questions that have a yes or no answer. You might often need to repeat or rephrase yourself, as your loved one might face difficulty understanding, so don’t get agitated or frustrated. Lastly, keep your tone gentle and friendly when communicating with them.
