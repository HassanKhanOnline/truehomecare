Sundowning in Dementia: 5 Strategies for Calmer Evenings

Saima Adil Zafar

1 January, 2026

Summary

  • Sundowning in dementia causes confusion and agitation in the late afternoon and evening, impacting both individuals and caregivers.
  • Common causes include fatigue, environmental changes, disrupted circadian rhythms, and physical discomfort, leading to increased anxiety.
  • 5 strategies to manage sundowning: establish routine, ensure proper lighting, limit stimulants, engage in calming activities, and check for discomfort.
  • Seek professional help if symptoms worsen, and contact True Homecare for specialized dementia care and support.

Introduction

Dementia is a condition that impacts not only the individual affected but also their family members, caregivers, and loved ones. One of the most challenging and distressing behaviors for families to cope with is sundowning in dementia. It refers to the increased confusion, agitation, and behavioral changes that many individuals with dementia experience in the late afternoon and evening hours. While sundowning can be distressing for both the client and their family, understanding it and learning strategies to manage it can significantly improve the quality of life for everyone involved.

In this blog, we will delve into sundowning in dementia, answering common questions about it, explaining why it happens, and offering practical strategies to manage it. By the end, we hope to provide some clarity on how you can reduce the effects of sundowning and help both your loved one and your family enjoy calmer evenings.

What is Sundowning in Dementia?

Sundowning refers to the phenomenon in which individuals with dementia become more agitated, confused, or exhibit disruptive behavior as the day progresses into the evening. This can include restlessness, difficulty sleeping, irritability, and even aggression. The condition tends to peak in the late afternoon and evening, which is why it’s sometimes called “sundowners and dementia.”

For family members and caregivers, sundowning can be challenging. As the person’s confusion intensifies, it may feel impossible to calm them down. Furthermore, sundowners symptoms can vary widely, making it difficult to predict how someone will behave during this time. Understanding the underlying causes of these behaviors is key to developing strategies for handling them.

What Stage of Dementia is Sundowning?

The question often arises: What stage of dementia is sundowning most commonly observed? Sundowning is most frequently associated with the later stages of dementia, though it can occur at any point in the progression of the condition. It is more pronounced in moderate to severe stages, where the person has significant memory impairment and confusion.

However, it’s important to note that not all individuals with dementia will experience sundowning. Some may have mild symptoms, while others may never exhibit any at all. The behavior is particularly common in individuals with Alzheimer’s disease, but it can also affect those with other forms of dementia, including Parkinson’s disease.

Why Do Dementia Clients Get Angry or Agitated at Night?

A common concern for family members is, why do dementia clients get angry at night? The answer is multifaceted. Several factors contribute to increased agitation and frustration during the evening:

  • Fatigue: By the end of the day, the individual may be exhausted, which can lead to heightened irritability and difficulty managing emotions.
  • Changes in Environment: As the day turns into night, lighting and surroundings can change, making it harder for the person to orient themselves. This confusion can trigger anxiety and agitation in dementia patients.
  • Internal Clocks: Many individuals with dementia have disruptions to their circadian rhythm, which can lead to a sense of disorientation in the evening.
  • Physical Discomfort: As the day progresses, some people with dementia may experience physical discomfort such as pain, hunger, or a need for the bathroom. These sensations can lead to agitation, especially when the person can’t communicate their discomfort effectively.

Understanding the root causes of sundowning can help caregivers approach the situation with empathy, reducing the likelihood of confrontational or distressing behaviors.

What Causes Agitation in Dementia Clients?

The agitation often seen in clients with dementia can be caused by a variety of factors, including cognitive impairment, sensory issues, and environmental changes. What causes agitation in dementia patients can vary depending on the individual and the stage of dementia they are in.

Here are a few key contributors to agitation:

  • Loss of ability to communicate needs: As dementia progresses, the individual may struggle to express themselves, leading to frustration and agitation. This is why it’s essential to offer emotional support and maintain patience when interacting with your loved one.
  • Unfamiliar environments: If the client is in a new environment or setting, this can trigger confusion and anxiety, particularly in the evening when the person may feel less secure.
  • Medication side effects: Some medications used to treat dementia can have side effects that increase agitation, especially during the evening hours. Consulting with healthcare professionals to review medications may help identify the cause.
  • Decreased sensory stimulation: As the day transitions into night, there may be a reduction in visual and auditory stimuli. This can lead to feelings of unease and, in turn, contribute to sundowning.

5 Strategies to Manage Sundowning in Dementia

As a caregiver, managing sundowning in dementia and how to calm gitated dementia patient can feel overwhelming, but there are several strategies you can use to help your loved one enjoy a calmer evening. Here are five strategies that can make a significant difference: You may also find our tips for dementia caregivers helpful for day-to-day support and reducing stress for the whole family.

1. Establish a Consistent Routine

One of the best ways to reduce sundowning symptoms is to create a predictable routine. Consistency in daily activities, including mealtimes, medication schedules, and sleep routines, can help alleviate anxiety and confusion for the individual. When the person with dementia knows what to expect, they may feel more secure and less likely to experience agitation in the evening.

2. Provide Adequate Lighting

As evening approaches, lighting can significantly impact the individual’s sense of security. Ensure that rooms are well-lit, and avoid dim or dark spaces that may increase confusion and fear. Sundowning can often be worsened by poor visibility, making it harder for the individual to recognize their surroundings or family members. Bright, soft lighting can help create a calm and safe atmosphere.

3. Limit Stimulants in the Evening

Caffeine, sugar, and heavy meals can contribute to sleep disturbances and sundowners symptoms. Avoid serving caffeine or sugar-laden snacks in the late afternoon or evening, and ensure that the person with dementia eats a light dinner. A calming herbal tea or a warm bath before bed can also help prepare the individual for a more restful night.

4. Offer Calming Activities

Engaging in relaxing activities during the late afternoon and evening can help distract from anxiety or aggression. Consider taking the person on a gentle walk in the park, or doing a calming activity like reading together or listening to soothing music. Keeping their mind engaged with positive stimuli may help reduce feelings of fear or aggression. Try rotating gentle, familiar options from a list of activities for dementia patients.

5. Monitor for Discomfort

Pain or discomfort can often go unnoticed in individuals with dementia, particularly in the later stages. Be sure to check for signs of discomfort, such as facial grimacing or restlessness, and address these issues promptly. How to calm an agitated dementia client often requires a compassionate approach, addressing both physical and emotional needs.

When to Seek Professional Help for Sundowning in Dementia

If you find that your loved one’s sundowning symptoms are becoming increasingly difficult to manage, it may be time to seek professional help. Working alongside other healthcare professionals like district nurses, GPs, hospitals, and pharmacists can help provide additional support and ensure that all health needs are being met. Conclusion

Managing sundowning in dementia requires a combination of understanding, patience, and practical strategies. By establishing a consistent routine, adjusting the environment, and providing calming activities, you can help reduce the intensity of sundowners symptoms and create a more peaceful evening for both your loved one and your family. It’s essential to remember that sundowning is a common part of dementia but can be managed with the right approach.
